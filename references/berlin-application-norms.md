# German and Berlin application norms

Reviewed: 2026-07-12. Re-check annually.

This is what US-centric CV tools get wrong. A German application is a different object.

## The Lebenslauf (CV)

- **Tabular**, reverse-chronological, **max 2 A4 pages**. Not a US-style prose résumé.
- Month/year for every station. Gaps are noticed; a short honest line beats a hidden hole.
- Sections: personal data, work experience, education, skills, languages. Hobbies optional.
- Usual to include date/place of birth and address. **Omit** marital status and religion.

### The photo question

Get this right, it is the classic tell.

- A photo is **not legally required.** The Federal Employment Agency now says so explicitly, and
  under the AGG a job ad may not demand one.
- It is still **culturally common**, especially at traditional German employers.
- At **international Berlin startups it is increasingly optional and often skipped.**

So: **never tell the candidate they must include a photo.** Tell them it is optional, more
expected in traditional German settings than in an English-speaking Berlin startup, and let them
decide. If they include one, it should be professional and current.

## The Anschreiben (cover letter)

Still a normal part of a German application — the BA and Make it in Germany both treat cover
letter + CV + certificates as the standard set. But **do not force one where the employer clearly
does not want it.**

The rule:

- The posting asks for a letter, asks for motivation, or the employer is a traditional German
  company → **write the Anschreiben.** One page, formal structure, tailored, concrete about why
  this company.
- The Berlin startup's ATS form is minimal and asks targeted free-text questions instead → **put
  the effort into those answers.** A ceremonial PDF letter nobody asked for is wasted work.

## Arbeitszeugnis (employer reference)

A German object with no US equivalent, and a place to be careful.

- §109 GewO gives every employee the right to a written certificate on leaving, and it **bans
  wording carrying a hidden meaning** not apparent from the text.
- In practice a coded "Zeugnissprache" persists anyway. "stets zu unserer vollsten Zufriedenheit"
  is top marks; drop a word and the grade drops with it.

**What you do:** if the candidate has Zeugnisse, offer to **decode and explain** the phrasing so
they know what a prospective employer will read. Explain the convention.

**What you never do:** deliver a confident verdict on a phrase you are not certain about, or tell
them their Zeugnis is "bad" on a hunch. Say what the convention suggests, flag the uncertainty,
and point them at a lawyer or works council if it looks materially damaging.

## LinkedIn vs Xing

LinkedIn has overtaken Xing in Germany and is the primary network for Berlin tech and for
international candidates. Xing retains some relevance for traditional German SMEs. For a Berlin
startup search: **LinkedIn.** Do not send someone to build a Xing profile for a Berlin startup job.

## The English-vs-German decision

The single most valuable call you make. See SKILL.md for the rule. The key asymmetry:

- ~**56%** of Berlin startups use English as their working language.
- But nationally only ~**2.7%** of German job ads say German is not required.

Berlin startups are a genuine exception to the German labour market — **not evidence that Germany
is English-friendly.** Never generalise from one to the other. If the candidate is looking outside
the Berlin startup bubble, the German expectation snaps back hard, and they need to know that.
